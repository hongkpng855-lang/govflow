## Article Title

**Significant Controllers Register (Hong Kong)**

---

## Article Body

The **Significant Controllers Register (SCR)** is a statutory register that most companies incorporated in Hong Kong must maintain under the Companies Ordinance (Cap. 622). It records individuals or legal entities that have significant control over a company. The requirement was introduced by the Companies (Amendment) Ordinance 2018 and came into effect on 1 March 2018.<ref>{{cite web |title=Companies Registry — Significant Controllers Register Overview |url=https://www.cr.gov.hk/en/legislation/scr/overview.htm |publisher=Hong Kong Companies Registry |access-date=22 June 2026}}</ref>

### Purpose

The SCR was introduced to enhance the transparency of corporate beneficial ownership in Hong Kong, fulfilling the jurisdiction's international obligations as a member of the Financial Action Task Force (FATF).<ref>{{cite web |title=Enhancing Transparency of Beneficial Ownership of Hong Kong Companies |url=https://www.cr.gov.hk/en/publications/docs/speech20171129-e.pdf |publisher=Hong Kong Companies Registry |date=29 November 2017}}</ref> The register enables law enforcement agencies — including the Hong Kong Police, the Independent Commission Against Corruption (ICAC), the Inland Revenue Department, and the Securities and Futures Commission — to access beneficial ownership information in a timely manner during investigations.<ref name="oln">{{cite web |title=The Beneficial Ownership of Hong Kong Companies |url=https://oln-law.com/the-beneficial-ownership-of-hong-kong-companies/ |publisher=Oldham, Li & Nie |date=24 July 2017}}</ref>

The SCR is not a public register. Only law enforcement officers and the significant controllers whose names appear on the register may inspect it.<ref name="statrys">{{cite web |title=Guide to Significant Controllers Register in Hong Kong |url=https://statrys.com/hk/guides/company-formation/significant-controllers-register-hong-kong |publisher=Statrys |date=2025}}</ref>

### Definition of a Significant Controller

A person (natural or legal) has significant control over a company if they meet one or more of the following conditions:<ref>{{cite web |title=Companies Ordinance (Cap. 622) — Schedule 5A |url=https://www.cr.gov.hk/en/legislation/companies-ordinance/cap622/companies-ordinance.htm |publisher=Hong Kong Companies Registry}}</ref>

1. Holds, directly or indirectly, more than 25% of the issued shares in the company (or, if the company has no share capital, holds the right to share in more than 25% of its capital or profits).
2. Holds, directly or indirectly, more than 25% of the voting rights of the company.
3. Holds the right to appoint or remove a majority of the board of directors.
4. Has the right to exercise, or actually exercises, significant influence or control over the company.
5. Has the right to exercise, or actually exercises, significant influence or control over the activities of a trust or firm that is not a legal person, whose trustees or members satisfy any of the first four conditions in relation to the company.

### Scope of Application

The SCR requirement applies to all companies incorporated in Hong Kong under the Companies Ordinance, including companies limited by shares, companies limited by guarantee, and unlimited companies.<ref name="gov-speech" />

The following are exempt:<ref>{{cite web |title=Companies (Amendment) Ordinance 2018 — Frequently Asked Questions |url=https://www.cr.gov.hk/en/legislation/scr/faq.htm |publisher=Hong Kong Companies Registry}}</ref>

* Companies whose shares are listed on the Stock Exchange of Hong Kong (which are subject to separate disclosure requirements under the Securities and Futures Ordinance).
* Non-Hong Kong companies that have registered branches or representative offices in Hong Kong under Part 16 of the Companies Ordinance.

### Contents of the Register

The SCR must contain the following information for each significant controller:<ref>{{cite web |title=Companies Ordinance (Cap. 622) — Schedule 5B |url=https://www.cr.gov.hk/en/legislation/companies-ordinance/cap622/companies-ordinance.htm |publisher=Hong Kong Companies Registry}}</ref>

**For a natural person:**
* Full name
* Communication address (not a post office box)
* Hong Kong identity card number or, if none, passport number and issuing country
* Date on which the person became a registrable person
* Nature of control over the company

**For a legal entity:**
* Name
* Registration number (or equivalent) in its place of incorporation
* Registered office or principal office address
* Legal form and governing law
* Date on which it became a registrable legal entity
* Nature of control over the company

**Designated representative:**
The company must also appoint at least one designated representative to provide assistance relating to the SCR to law enforcement officers. The representative must be:<ref>{{cite web |title=Guidelines on the Keeping of Significant Controllers Register |url=https://www.cr.gov.hk/en/publications/docs/Guidelines_scr_e.pdf |publisher=Hong Kong Companies Registry |format=PDF}}</ref>

* A member, director, or employee of the company who is a natural person resident in Hong Kong; or
* An accounting professional, legal professional, or licensed trust or company service provider (as defined under the Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorist Financing Ordinance, Cap. 615).

### Company Obligations

Under the Companies (Amendment) Ordinance 2018, companies must:<ref name="gov-speech">{{cite web |title=Legislative Council Brief — Companies (Amendment) Bill 2017 |url=https://www.cr.gov.hk/en/publications/docs/speech20171129-e.pdf |publisher=Hong Kong Companies Registry |date=29 November 2017}}</ref>

* **Keep the SCR** at the company's registered office or a prescribed place within Hong Kong. If the register is not kept at the registered office, the company must file a notice (Form NR2) with the Companies Registry within 14 days<ref name="bbcincorp">{{cite web |title=Hong Kong's Significant Controllers Register: How Does It Work? |url=https://bbcincorp.com/hk/articles/hong-kong-significant-controllers-register |publisher=BBCIncorp |date=2025}}</ref> specifying the location.
* **Take reasonable steps** to identify significant controllers, including issuing notices to persons whom the company knows or has reasonable cause to believe are significant controllers.
* **Enter required particulars** into the SCR within 7 days of receiving confirmation from a significant controller.
* **Keep the register up to date**, entering any changes to particulars within 7 days of becoming aware of them.<ref name="arnifi">{{cite web |title=Significant Controllers Register Hong Kong — Compliance for Private Companies |url=https://arnifi.com/blog/significant-controllers-register-in-hong-kong-guide |publisher=Arnifi |date=2025}}</ref>
* **Make the SCR available** for inspection by law enforcement officers at any reasonable time.

If a company knows or has reasonable cause to believe that it has no significant controller, this fact must still be stated in the SCR.<ref name="fastlane">{{cite web |title=Significant Controllers Register Update Guidance |url=https://fastlane-global.com/zh-hk/blog/hk-company-significant-controllers-register |publisher=Fastlane Group |date=2025}}</ref>

### Penalties for Non-Compliance

Failure to comply with SCR obligations is a criminal offence. The company and each of its responsible persons (officers in default) are liable upon conviction to:<ref>{{cite Hong Kong legislation | ordinance=Companies Ordinance | section=653(4) | cap=622}}</ref>

* A fine of up to HK$25,000 (level 4 fine), and
- A daily fine of up to HK$700 for each day the offence continues.

In addition, any person who knowingly or recklessly makes a false, misleading, or deceptive statement in the SCR commits an offence punishable by a fine of up to HK$300,000 and imprisonment for up to 2 years.<ref>{{cite Hong Kong legislation | ordinance=Companies Ordinance | section=656(5) | cap=622}}</ref>

### Background and Legislative History

Hong Kong's company law did not originally require disclosure of beneficial ownership information. The register of members, kept under section 627 of the Companies Ordinance, showed only the legal owners of shares. This created a transparency gap that the FATF identified during its mutual evaluation of Hong Kong.<ref>{{cite web |title=FATF — Anti-money laundering and counter-terrorist financing measures: Hong Kong, China |url=https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/publications/Mutualevaluations/mer-hong-kong-2019.html |publisher=Financial Action Task Force |date=2019}}</ref>

The Companies (Amendment) Bill 2017 was introduced into the Legislative Council on 28 June 2017. It was enacted and commenced on 1 March 2018.<ref name="oln" />

A 2019 study by the Hong Kong Companies Registry found that approximately 12% of inspected companies had SCR-related deficiencies, leading to enhanced enforcement efforts in subsequent years.<ref>{{cite web |title=Companies Registry Annual Report 2019–2020 |url=https://www.cr.gov.hk/en/publications/ann.htm |publisher=Hong Kong Companies Registry}}</ref>

### Comparison with Other Jurisdictions

The SCR is Hong Kong's equivalent of the UK's Persons with Significant Control (PSC) register, which was introduced in 2016. Key differences include:<ref>{{cite web |title=Beneficial Ownership Registers: Regulation Around the World |url=https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en-hk/knowledge/publications/abe55ea5/beneficial-ownership-registers-regulation-around-the-world |publisher=Norton Rose Fulbright |date=March 2023}}</ref>

| Feature | Hong Kong SCR | UK PSC Register |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| Public access | No — law enforcement only | Yes — publicly searchable |
| Threshold | 25% ownership or control | 25% ownership or control |
| Introduced | 1 March 2018 | 6 April 2016 |
| Filing with central register | No (company-level only) | Yes (Companies House) |

Unlike the UK register, which is freely accessible online through Companies House, the Hong Kong SCR is a private company-level record. This distinction has been noted by transparency advocates, though the Hong Kong government has maintained that law enforcement access suffices for anti-money-laundering purposes.<ref>{{cite news |title=Hong Kong Keeps Beneficial Ownership Register Private in New Law |url=https://www.reuters.com |work=Reuters |date=2017}}</ref>
